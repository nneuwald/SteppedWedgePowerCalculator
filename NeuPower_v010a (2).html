Outcome type
Continuous outcomes are measured on a numeric scale (e.g. test scores, blood pressure, weight). Binary outcomes have only two possible results (e.g. responded/did not respond, relapsed/did not relapse). The choice affects which statistical test is used and how effect sizes are defined.
Within-subject designs
Each participant receives both the treatment and control conditions at different times, serving as their own control. This eliminates stable between-person differences from the analysis, making these designs substantially more efficient than parallel designs for the same sample size. Crossover trials alternate conditions within a single study period. Stepped wedge and delayed-start designs stagger when groups cross over from control to treatment.
Each participant receives both treatment and control in two separate phases (AB/BA sequences). The order is counterbalanced to eliminate period effects. Because each person is their own control, between-person variance cancels, making this the most efficient design when treatment effects are reversible. Best for: short-acting interventions with no carryover (e.g. analgesics, cognitive tasks, acute drug effects).
Groups of participants (cohorts) start in the control condition and sequentially cross over to treatment at staggered time points. Every cohort eventually receives treatment, which can be ethically advantageous. Classic stepped wedge uses the H&H framework; delayed-start gives each cohort equal treatment duration. Best for: interventions that cannot be withdrawn, policy rollouts, or when withholding treatment long-term is impractical.
Parallel designs
Participants are assigned to one condition only (treatment or control) and stay in that group for the entire study. Between-person variability remains in the analysis, so these designs typically require larger sample sizes than within-subject designs. However, they are simpler to implement and avoid concerns about treatment carryover or ordering effects.
The standard two-arm randomized controlled trial. Participants are randomly assigned to treatment or control and measured one or more times. The simplest and most widely understood design. Best for: disease-modifying or irreversible treatments, when carryover is a concern, or when the primary goal is regulatory approval with a straightforward analysis.
Power analysis for trials where the primary endpoint is time until an event occurs (death, relapse, progression). Uses the log-rank test / Cox proportional hazards framework. Power depends on the number of events observed, not just sample size. Requires specifying hazard ratio, accrual period, and follow-up duration. Best for: oncology, cardiology, any trial measuring duration until a clinical event.
Randomization occurs at the group level (e.g. clinics, schools, communities) rather than the individual level. All participants within a cluster receive the same condition. The design effect (DEFF) inflates the required sample size because within-cluster similarity reduces effective information. Best for: interventions delivered at the group level (e.g. training programs, policy changes, community health interventions).
Tests whether a new treatment is not meaningfully worse than an existing one, within a pre-specified margin. Uses one-sided testing. The goal is to show the new treatment is "good enough" while potentially offering other advantages (fewer side effects, lower cost, easier administration). Best for: generic drugs, biosimilars, or new treatments expected to match (not beat) the standard of care.
Tests whether two treatments are equivalent within a pre-specified margin (±Δ) using Two One-Sided Tests (TOST). Both tests must reject to conclude equivalence. Unlike NI testing (which only checks one direction), TOST confirms the difference is bounded in BOTH directions. Best for: bioequivalence studies, generic drug approval, validating measurement instruments, or any context where you need to prove two treatments produce essentially the same result.
Tests two interventions simultaneously by crossing them (A vs no-A) × (B vs no-B), creating 4 cells. Main effects use all participants (efficient), but interaction tests have less power. Best for: testing two interventions that can be combined (e.g. drug + therapy, exercise + diet), especially when interaction is not the primary question.

Hazard ratio (HR)
The hazard ratio compares how quickly events happen in the treatment group versus the control group. An HR of 0.70 means treated participants experience the event (death, relapse, etc.) at 70% the rate of control — in other words, a 30% reduction in risk. An HR of 0.50 means a 50% reduction (the treatment cuts the event rate in half). Smaller HR = bigger treatment benefit = fewer total events needed to prove it works.
Median survival, control (months)
Median time to event in the control arm, in months. This is how long 50% of control participants survive before experiencing the event. Used to calculate the baseline hazard rate under an exponential survival model.
Accrual period (months)
Duration of participant enrollment in months. Participants enter the study uniformly over this period. Longer accrual means more variable follow-up times across participants.
Minimum follow-up (months)
Minimum follow-up time after the last participant is enrolled, in months. The total study duration is accrual + follow-up. Longer follow-up means more events are observed, improving power.

Calendar-anchored: All cohorts are assessed at shared calendar time points (e.g. Jan, Feb, Mar). The model includes a time covariate to adjust for secular trends (maturation, seasonal effects, practice effects). This is more conservative but protects against time-varying confounds. The treatment effect is interpreted as the improvement beyond what would have happened due to time alone.

Rolling (unchecked): Each cohort's assessments are relative to their own crossover point. No shared time axis. The model omits the time covariate, which yields higher power but assumes no systematic time trends. The treatment effect is interpreted as the within-person change from baseline. Choose rolling when enrollment is staggered and calendar time is not meaningful.
Cluster autocorrelation (CAC)
What is this? In a stepped wedge trial, each cohort (group) crosses over from control to treatment at a different time. CAC measures how consistent each cohort's performance is from one period to the next.

When does this matter? It depends on what your cohorts represent:

If your cohorts are natural clusters (clinics, hospitals, schools, wards) — each site has its own culture, staffing, and patient mix that can shift over time. A clinic that outperforms in January may not outperform in June. Use CAC = 0.7–0.9. Longer studies or more variable settings → lower CAC.

If your cohorts are randomly assigned groups of individuals (not tied to a site) — there is little reason for cohort-level effects to persist, and CAC has minimal impact on power. Use CAC = 1.0 (the default).

Quick guide:
• 1.0 — Standard assumption. Cohort effects are perfectly stable. Use for short studies or random groups.
• 0.8 — Recommended conservative default for site-based clusters spanning several months.
• 0.6–0.7 — Substantial drift. Use for multi-year studies or sites with high staff turnover.

Lower CAC reduces power, sometimes substantially. If unsure, try 0.8 and compare with 1.0 using the scenario comparison feature.

Reference: Hooper, Teerenstra, de Hoop & Eldridge (2016). Sample size calculation for stepped wedge and other longitudinal cluster randomised trials. Statistics in Medicine, 35(26), 4718–4728.

Carryover proportion (λ)
Carryover (λ) is the fraction of the treatment effect that persists into the next period after washout. It attenuates the crossover contrast by λ/2.

λ = 0 Perfect washout. No residual treatment effect. Use when the washout period is long relative to the drug's half-life (≥ 5 half-lives), or for behavioural/educational interventions with no physiological persistence.
0.05 – 0.15 Minimal carryover. Adequate washout for most pharmacological agents. Common for short-acting drugs with a washout of 3–5 half-lives.
0.15 – 0.30 Moderate carryover. Suboptimal washout, slow-clearance drugs, or treatments with lingering physiological effects (e.g., steroids, SSRIs). Consider extending the washout period.
0.30 – 0.50 Substantial carryover. Disease-modifying agents, long-acting biologics, or surgical/device interventions. Power loss becomes meaningful; a parallel design may be preferable.
> 0.50 Severe carryover. The treatment fundamentally alters the baseline state (e.g., gene therapy, transplant). A crossover design is likely inappropriate; consider a parallel or delayed-start design instead.

How to estimate λ: If pilot or PK data is available, λ ≈ Cresidual / Cpeak at the start of period 2, where C is the plasma concentration (or biomarker effect). Without data, use a conservative estimate (0.10–0.20) as a sensitivity analysis.

When a study tests more than one hypothesis — for example, two primary endpoints, both main effects in a factorial, or multiple subgroup comparisons — each test at α=0.05 increases the chance of a false positive. With 3 independent tests at α=0.05, there is a ~14% chance of at least one false positive instead of 5%.

A multiple comparison correction lowers the per-test significance threshold so the overall false positive rate stays at the intended α. This reduces power, meaning you need more participants to detect the same effect.
Tests
The number of confirmatory hypotheses in your study.

2: Two co-primary endpoints, or both main effects in a factorial.
3: Both main effects + interaction in a factorial, or three co-primary endpoints.
4+: Multiple dose levels, subgroups, or endpoints. Type a number up to 20.

Only count pre-specified confirmatory analyses. Exploratory or secondary analyses don't need correction here.
Method
Divides your significance level by the number of tests: α/k.

With α=0.05 and 2 tests, each test uses α=0.025. With 3 tests, each uses α=0.0167.

This is the most widely used correction. It is conservative — it guarantees the overall false positive rate stays below α no matter how the tests are correlated. Because it is conservative, it may slightly overestimate the sample size needed.
Uses the formula 1−(1−α)1/k to compute the per-test threshold.

With α=0.05 and 2 tests, each test uses α≈0.0253 (vs 0.025 for Bonferroni). With 3 tests, α≈0.0170 (vs 0.0167).

Slightly less conservative than Bonferroni, giving marginally more power. The difference is small in practice. Technically exact when the tests are independent, and still valid when tests are positively correlated (which is the usual case in clinical trials).

If you never observe how participants change during the control phase, you can't attribute post-crossover improvement to treatment rather than time. Quick Start Guide
1. Choose your design

Use the toolbar at the top to select your outcome type (Continuous or Binary) and study design (Crossover, Stepped Wedge, Simple RCT, etc.).

2. Set your parameters

Adjust the sliders in the left panel to match your expected treatment effect, variability, ICC, sample size, and number of assessments. Click any ? button for an explanation of that parameter.

3. Read your results

The stat cards at the top of the main panel update in real time. They show your Total N, estimated Power, effect size (with minimum detectable effect), and the minimum N needed for your target power. The Power card includes a Simulate button for Monte Carlo verification and an Auto-verify checkbox that automatically checks whether the analytical formula is accurate for your configuration.

4. Explore the sections

Click the collapsible headers to reveal:

Design matrix — visual layout of your study. Drag cells to customize timing.

Design comparison — approximate N across all design types. Click any bar to switch.

Power visualized — bell curve distributions and variance breakdown.

Sensitivity table — min N across ICC × effect size combinations. Click any cell to apply those parameters.

Show the math — step-by-step worked calculation.

Summary — grant-ready paragraph describing your power analysis.

GPower & R code — verify results externally with step-by-step GPower instructions and exportable R code (including Monte Carlo simulation).

5. Compare scenarios

Click + Save scenario below the stat cards to snapshot your current configuration. Save up to 6 scenarios, then click Compare ▼ to view them side-by-side in a table. Click any chip to restore that configuration. Double-click a chip name to rename it.

Tips

• Click the sigmoid logo to reset parameters to defaults.

• Use A−/A+ to zoom the interface.

• Use Share configuration to save or share your exact setup.

• Enable Auto-verify in the Power card to automatically detect and correct inaccurate analytical results. NeuPower will warn you when this is recommended.

• Enable Multiple comparisons in Study Parameters to adjust α for multiple testing (Bonferroni or Šidák).

• For clustered RCT designs, adjust Cluster size variability (CV) to account for unequal cluster sizes.

• For stepped wedge designs, adjust Cluster autocorrelation (CAC) to model temporal instability in cluster effects.

• Use Ctrl+P (or Cmd+P) to print a clean report of your current power analysis.

• All calculations update instantly as you move sliders.

What is NeuPower

A sample size and power calculator for clinical trials. Eight designs — crossover, stepped wedge, delayed-start, parallel RCT, clustered RCT, non-inferiority, equivalence (TOST), survival, and factorial 2×2 — all computed under a unified GLS framework, with continuous and binary outcomes. Built-in Monte Carlo simulation automatically verifies every calculation and corrects the displayed power when the analytical formula is inaccurate. Everything runs in the browser. No install, no account, no data leaves your machine.

Why use NeuPower

Most power tools handle one design at a time with separate interfaces and incompatible assumptions. NeuPower lets you set your effect size, variance, and ICC once, then instantly see the sample size implications across all eight designs. A crossover might need 40 participants where a parallel RCT needs 300 — that comparison takes seconds here and hours anywhere else.

Several designs supported here — delayed-start, cohort-anchored stepped wedge, custom stagger patterns — have no equivalent in GPower, PASS, or standard R packages. NeuPower fills that gap with full matrix-based GLS computation and an interactive, editable design matrix that lets you drag observation cells and see power update in real time.

Nothing is opaque. Every result can be verified with automatic Monte Carlo simulation (all eight designs), which detects and corrects cases where the analytical formula is inaccurate — binary clustered designs, extreme event rates, few clusters, and small samples. Exportable R code (analytical + simulation), step-by-step GPower instructions, and a formula breakdown show exactly how the SE and power were derived.

The grant summary (with inline methodological citations), sensitivity table, and scenario comparison are structured for direct inclusion in an NIH, PCORI, or foundation power analysis section — ready to paste, not ready to rewrite.

Key assumptions

Compound symmetry covariance with optional cluster autocorrelation decay for stepped wedge (Hooper et al., 2016). Normal approximation (z-test) for analytical formula; automatic Monte Carlo simulation verification detects and corrects inaccuracies. Two-sided α for superiority; one-sided for NI; TOST for equivalence. Binary outcomes use a linearized risk-difference approximation. Survival uses the Schoenfeld (1983) log-HR variance with competing-risk dropout. Clustered RCT supports unequal cluster sizes via coefficient of variation (CV). Dropout modeled as reduced effective N. Multiple comparisons corrected via Bonferroni or Šidák.
